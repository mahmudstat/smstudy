2  GRE Word List

2.1 A

  1. Aberrant: Markedly different from an accepted norm.
  2. Aberration: A deviation from the normal or typical course, often of a negative nature.
  3. Abstain: To deliberately avoid doing something, especially something that is considered unhealthy or immoral.
  4. Abstruse: Difficult to understand; obscure.
  5. Adamant: Unyielding in attitude or opinion, especially in the face of adversity or persuasion.
  6. Admonish: To warn or reprimand someone firmly.
  7. Admonitory: Giving a warning or reprimand.
  8. Aesthete: A person who has a deep appreciation for art, beauty, or nature.
  9. Aesthetic: Relating to the appreciation of beauty or art.
  10. Aesthetic: Concerned with the nature of beauty or artistic taste.
  11. Amalgam: A mixture or combination of different elements.
  12. Ambiguous: Having more than one possible meaning; unclear or inexact.
  13. Ambivalent: Having mixed feelings or contradictory ideas about something or someone.
  14. Ameliorate: To make something better or improve its condition.
  15. Amenable: Open and responsive to suggestion or advice; willing to be influenced.
  16. Amorphous: Lacking a clear or definite shape or structure.
  17. Anomalous: Deviating from the normal or expected.
  18. Anomaly: A deviation from what is standard, normal, or expected.
  19. Antipathy: A deep-seated feeling of dislike or aversion.
  20. Antithetical: Directly opposed or contrasted; mutually incompatible.
  21. Apathetic: Showing or feeling little or no emotion or interest.
  22. Apathy: Lack of interest, enthusiasm, or concern.
  23. Apocryphal: Of doubtful authenticity, although widely circulated as being true.
  24. Appease: To calm, soothe, or bring peace to; to placate or satisfy.
  25. Arbitrary: Based on random choice or personal whim, rather than any reason or system.
  26. Arcane: Understood by few; mysterious or secret.
  27. Arduous: Involving a lot of effort and difficulty; strenuous.
  28. Artful: Skillful or clever, especially in a deceitful or cunning way.
  29. Artful: Having or showing creative skill; cunning or crafty.
  30. Ascetic: Practicing severe self-discipline and abstention from all forms of indulgence, typically for religious reasons.
  31. Ascetic: Characterized by self-denial and austere living.
  32. Askance: With suspicion, mistrust, or disapproval.
  33. Audacious: Bold, daring, or willing to take risks, often in a reckless or disrespectful way.
  34. Audacity: Excessive boldness or daring, often with disregard for others’ opinions or safety.
  35. Auspicious: Indicating a favorable or successful outcome; promising good fortune.
  36. Austere: Severe or strict in manner, attitude, or appearance; simple and unadorned.
  37. Austere: Harsh or severe in disposition or nature; lacking in decoration or luxury.
  38. Austere: Strict and uncompromising; ascetic or self-disciplined.
  39. Avaricious: Having an insatiable desire for wealth or material gain; greedy.

2.2 B

  • Banal: So lacking in originality as to be obvious and boring.
  • Banality: The condition or quality of being dull or unoriginal; obvious or trite.
  • Belie: To fail to give a true impression of something; to contradict or misrepresent.
  • Belligerent: Hostile and aggressive; engaged in conflict or war.
  • Betray: To reveal or disclose something hidden, often by accident or in violation of trust; to be disloyal.
  • Blatant: Completely obvious, often offensively so; conspicuously offensive.
  • Bolster: To support, strengthen, or reinforce something.
  • Brazen: Bold and shameless; audacious in a way that is often disrespectful.
  • Bucolic: Relating to the countryside or rural life; pastoral.
  • Bumbling: Characterized by clumsiness or awkwardness, often in a way that causes problems.
  • Burgeon: To grow or develop rapidly; to flourish or increase quickly.

    • 2.3 C

  • Calumny: The making of false and defamatory statements about someone to damage their reputation.
  • Capricious: Given to sudden and unaccountable changes in mood or behavior.
  • Castigate: To reprimand or criticize severely.
  • Censure: To express severe disapproval of someone or something, typically in a formal statement.
  • Chastise: To reprimand or scold severely, often for moral or disciplinary reasons.
  • Chortle: To laugh in a gleeful, partly suppressed way.
  • Circumscribe: To restrict or limit something within certain boundaries.
  • Circumvent: To find a way around an obstacle or limitation, often by clever or deceitful means.
  • Commensurate: Corresponding in size, degree, or extent; proportionate.
  • Concede: To admit or acknowledge something, often reluctantly; to surrender or yield.
  • Concede: To yield or grant something as a right or privilege.
  • Concede: To accept the truth or validity of something after denying it initially.
  • Confound: To cause confusion or surprise, especially by being unexpected.
  • Confound: To mix up or confuse, often to the point of bewilderment.
  • Conspicuous: Clearly visible or easily noticed; attracting attention.
  • Constituent: A component or part of something; a person who authorizes another to act on their behalf.
  • Constituent: A member of a group or body of voters who elect representatives.
  • Construe: To interpret or analyze something in a particular way.
  • Contingent: Dependent on or subject to chance or certain conditions; likely to occur but not certain.
  • Contingent: Dependent on or subject to chance or certain conditions; likely to occur but not certain.
  • Contrition: The state of feeling remorseful and repentant for one’s wrongdoings.
  • Contrive: To plan or create something through clever or ingenious methods, often in a deceptive way.
  • Copious: Abundant in quantity; large in number or amount.
  • Craven: Characterized by cowardice; lacking courage.
  • Cryptic: Having a hidden or ambiguous meaning; mysterious or obscure.
  • Culminate: To reach the highest point, final stage, or climax of something.
  • Culpability: The state of being guilty or responsible for something bad or wrong.

    • 2.4 D

  • Decorous: Conforming to good taste, proper etiquette, and dignity; polite.
  • Decorum: Appropriate behavior, conduct, or manners in a given situation.
  • Deferential: Showing respect and esteem for someone or something, often by yielding or submitting to their opinion or wishes.
  • Deleterious: Having a harmful or damaging effect on something or someone.
  • Delineate: To describe or portray something in detail, often with precision.
  • Demur: To raise objections or show reluctance, often in response to a proposal or request.
  • Denigrate: To criticize or speak ill of someone or something, often unfairly.
  • Denote: To indicate, signify, or be a symbol of something.
  • Derivative: Imitative of the work of another, usually lacking originality.
  • Derive: To obtain or receive something from a source or origin.
  • Derive: To trace the origin or source of something.
  • Dictatorial: Having a characteristic of or resembling a dictator; authoritarian and overbearing.
  • Didactic: Intended to teach or instruct, often in a moralizing or condescending way.
  • Diffident: Lacking self-confidence or assertiveness; shy or reserved.
  • Dilatory: Tending to delay or procrastinate; slow to take action.
  • Dilettante: A person who engages in an activity, especially the arts, in a superficial or amateurish way.
  • Disaffected: Feeling disillusioned or dissatisfied, especially with authority or social structures.
  • Discrete: Separate and distinct from one another; individually separate.
  • Disinterested: Not influenced by personal interest; impartial or unbiased.
  • Dispassionate: Not influenced by strong emotions or personal feelings; objective.
  • Disseminate: To spread or distribute information, knowledge, or ideas widely.
  • Dogmatic: Stubbornly opinionated, often in a way that is rigid or intolerant of other viewpoints.
  • Duress: Compulsion by threat or force; the use of threats or coercion to make someone act against their will.
